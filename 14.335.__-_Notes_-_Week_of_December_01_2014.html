Log 12:05PM

Right, so new week. Last night, I left it at trying to understand the effect of the DIV_IN/DEL mutation rate on the population, which seems pretty disruptive. I submitted some runs to get at this.

Separately, I started thinking about how to evaluate the similarity of the trait selection values (the histograms of female choosy display_c) between the paired allopatric populations. I considered several options:

  • Chi-Square
    • Unsuitable.
      • Some of my values are zero. Chi-square is a proportion, so 1/0 is infinity
      • Non-symmetric. How the hell can I compare two populations if the first vs. second matters? This is because Chi-Square assumes that one of the populations is a known distribution, which is not a valid assumption for my work
  • Power Divergence (related to Chi-Sq)
    • Unsuitable for same reasons as Chi-Square.
  • KS
    • Unsuitable.
      • My values are binned, not continuous.
  • Mutual Information
    • Unsuitable?
      • How do you compare difference of probability between two scalar values? They aren't binary. :/

So, what I'm REALLY trying to get at is the average probability that an organism from a given population will mate with an organism from the other population.

  • Sum square of differences?
    • Undesirable because it magnifies the differences between populations that DO share a trait at high values. In other words, it penalizes you equally if you have 100 vs 50, as opposed to 50 vs 0, which is an entirely different scenario.
  • Sum log of differences?
    • Undesirable because it magnifies the small differences at points where there just aren't very many displaying that trait. Those are more noise than signal.
  • Sum differences?
    • Too flat?
  • Sum probabilities?

So, I want to get at is what the probability of an organism getting to mate.

https://people.richland.edu/james/lecture/m170/ch05-rul.html

  • Assume that all organisms reproduce equally. That is, they show up in the birth chamber at the rates documented by the histogram.
  • Assume that the organisms are hard-match. Otherwise, things get SUPER FUCKING MESSY. :/ Hard match from here on in.
  • Assume that the organisms only go for a single trait. To do multiple traits, things get more complicated. Single-trait match for now.

Probability of an organism having a given trait P(t):

  • t - the count of the trait
  • T - the total count of all trait expressions. ( T = Sum(all t) )
  • P(t) = t/T

The organisms can't have more than one trait, but they must have 1 trait, so all the probabilities MUST add up to 1.

  • Sum( P(t) ) = 1 (this is because all the events are mutually exclusive, so you can just ADD the probabilities.)

Right, so what about the organisms on the other side? (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_probability_distribution)

  • Calculate the individual trait probabilities (per above)
  • For each trait, calculate the joint probability.
    • P(tab) = P(ta)*P(tb)

Fine, but what about an overall measure of the joint probabilities?

Can I take the mean? It's a distribution, right? But, it's not really normally distributed...

Or should I just sum the individual joint probabilities? Because I'm trying to get the overall probabilities of a given organism from population A mating with a given organism in Population B, which is the P(t1ab) or P(t2ab) or P(t3ab), etc.

So! The sum of the joint probabilities! :D YES YES YES

Software-wise, trivial. Going to make a little script now. :D
